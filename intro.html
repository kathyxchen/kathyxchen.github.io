Japanese Film as a Critique of Western Realist Cinema

An argument in 3 units.

J.A. Murphy, University of Florida

Purpose

This presentation is designed to introduce the reader to Noel Burch's To the Distant Observer to provide a starting point in understanding Western critical discussion of Japanese cinema.

Assumptions

We assume that Burch's 1979 book is a seminal work, around which the debate on Japanese film is still structured. Burch argues that a critique of Hollywood realist cinema is inscribed in Japanese film practice of the 1930's and 40's, for the Western critic. Anderson and Richie call To the Distant Observer "the most insightful account of Japanese cinema ever written" (The Japanese Film, they're not necessarily happy with his politics, though). Cited frequently through the 90's in studies of both Japanese literature and film, Burch's work still stands as "the most provocative study of the Japanese cinema in any language," (Mitsuhiro Yoshimoto, "The Difficulty of Being Radical" Boundary 2 (Fall 91)). As a point of departure for subsequent literary and film criticism, Burch's argument cannot be avoided by any serious student of Japanese culture.
