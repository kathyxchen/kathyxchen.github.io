    This was not always the case. Early film audiences in Europe and America, for instance, were said to enjoy having the film interrupted so that the apparatus could be explained to them. Think of the reaction in a movie theater nowadays if the smooth flow of the film is stopped for even a few seconds.

    For Burch, the decision to maximize and generalize the diegetic effect of a film, rather than treating its story world as one aspect among many, is an early and decisive step toward what he calls Western Cinema, which we call mainstream or Hollywood realist cinema.

    Note: It is often argued that this decision is dictated by bourgeois norms of representation.

    The transition from what Burch calls the primitive mode of viewing and understanding film, to Hollywood's realist cinema, is generally agreed to have been worked out in its most important forms by 1916. A second important aspect of this transition is the notion of linearity. By linearity, Burch means two things: that a scene unfolds once, in one direction, in a causal sequence, and that the spectator's attention is always directed to the part of a scene most important for advancing the narrative.

    As with maximizing the diegesis, the emphasis on linearity was neither an obvious nor a necessary choice for telling stories with the new film technology.

    All these devices—framing, camera manipulation, focus, and closeup—can be seen as ways to "point" to specific parts of the mise-en-scene. One can argue that these are effective in directing our attention because they draw on cognitive faculties of perception in human beings which are valid across cultures and generations. This may well be the case, but what is important for Burch's comparison of Japanese cinema with mainstream realist cinema is not only that these devices were developed to direct our attention, but also what they direct our attention to. In mainstream realist cinema, they direct our attention in an economical and unambiguous way to what is most important for advancing the linear narrative. We will find that this is a major difference for Burch between Japanese and Western cinema.

    A Cumberland Romance (1920) (CH 17.37)

    We have already identified Western Cinema as one in which a linear narrative is represented to maximize diegetic effect. And at the center of this film world, we will find human characters.

    These three characteristics of mainstream realist cinema might seem to be obvious, or even necessary, to the act of telling a story with film. We will find, though, that this is not the case with Japanese film. In fact, it is precisely to question the seeming naturalness of these characteristics of Western Narrative cinema that Burch studies Japanese film.

    Although early filmmakers probably didn't think of it this way, these three characteristics of Western Cinema can be thought of as goals the emerging film industry set for itself. Achieving these goals, namely linearity, maximum diegetic effect, and character-based causality, required a mode of representation that would enhance the sense of a story world while hiding production aspects of film, eliminate spatio-temporal ambiguity at all levels, and constantly focus the spectator's attention in the direction of the advancing narrative.

    We will now study this mode of representation, developed in American and European cinema from 1905-1917. It is sometimes called Classical Hollywood style. Since this style is still used in the overwhelming majority of TV and movies right up to the present day, some critics prefer to call it "mainstream" realist style.

    This mode of representation consists of a series of strategies involving cinematography, mise-en-scene, editing, lighting, etc. codified by 1917, which serves to focus the spectator's attention on the diegetic effect of a character-based causal narrative.

    The mainstream mode of representation is codified in three axes:
    a. continuity/discontinuity
    b. depth/surface
    c. centered/decentered
     We will now go through these axes one by one, and show how the codes developed serve to support one or more of the three characteristics of mainstream cinema we outlined previously (maximum diegesis, linearity, character-centered).

    We will begin with the axis of continuity/discontinuity. One problem faced by filmmakers in early 1900s was how to harness the organizing power of editing without disrupting the story and confusing the spectator. A number of techniques and rules were developed to enable the viewer to construct the story space and time across the discontinuity of the cut, and these were strictly codified to ensure a smooth comprehension so the viewer could concentrate on the story.


    The Maltese Falcon (1941) establishing shot (FA 7.31)

    Two of the most fundamental devices used to construct an unambiguous story space are the establishing shot in conjunction with the shot/reverse shot. This combination allows the spectator to follow characters' conversations across cuts, and through all manner of framing changes without losing a sense of where each shot falls in the story space. In the establishing shot from The Maltese Falcon, we see Effie approaching Sam's desk.

    The establishing shot not only gives us an immediate picture of the story space, it also sets out what is called the 180° line of action. If you draw a line through Sam and Effie, the camera may shoot from anywhere on this side of the line, but may not cross to the other side.

    In order to get into the habit of noticing this ubiquitous norm of camerawork, first note in the establishing shot which shoulder the characters have to the camera. (Here, Sam's left, Effie's right.) In all subsequent shots you see the same shoulder towards the camera. Another consequence of the 180° line is that we never see the fourth wall. Let's follow the conversation between Effie and Sam...


    Shot of Effie (FA 7.32)

    As the camera pulls to a medium close-up (MCU) of Effie, we note that it is again her right shoulder to the camera, and we instinctively realize she is talking to Sam, even though he isn't shown...


    Reverse shot of Sam (FA 7.33)

    In the reverse shot, we see a MCU of Sam, and we see that, just as in the establishing shot, his left shoulder is to the camera. Establishing shots may fix the story space by preceding the shot/reverse shot, or they may follow it to confirm our expectations.

    However, in this 1932 Hollywood movie we see a shot/reverse shot that violates the 180° norm...


    The Phantom of Crestwood (1932)

    In this shot, the wealthy heiress Jenny Wren has entered the vault room of a bank, and is signed in by the clerk. Note that the clerk's right shoulder, and Ms. Wren's left are toward the camera.


    The Phantom of Crestwood (1932) reverse shot

    In the reverse shot, we see the clerk's left shoulder, and Ms. Wren's right. What has happened is simply that the camera has been taken to the other side of a line drawn through the two women, breaking the 180° line established in the first shot.

    We have shown this violation of a norm of mainstream realist cinema by a Hollywood movie both to illustrate the application of the "shoulder rule," and to show that, when we speak of "norms," we do not mean rigid rules. You can verify the dominance of these norms, though, by applying the "shoulder rule" to a few TV shows tonight. The vast majority of shows will carefully adhere to the 180° line.

    The eyeline match has been called a veritable keystone of the Hollywood editing system. Its function is simple, and codified to teach us, when faced with characters looking offscreen, to expect that the next shot will show us what they are looking at. This is commonly reinforced by such devices as the establishing shot and dialogue. We can illustrate this with a scene from the 1946 classic It's a Wonderful Life...

    In the first shot, we see Vi walking down the street, looking offscreen and addressing unseen characters. The establishing shot...


    Establishing shot (RR 205)

    shows Vi walking in front of George and the Policeman. This both fixes the story space and shows us who Vi was addressing, thereby confirming the clues from the dialogue. Drawing the 180° line between Vi and George, we note that Vi's right shoulder, and George's left are always on the same side of the camera. The next shots will illustrate the eyeline match. Here, we have two characters, George and the Policeman, looking off screen.


    George and the Policeman looking surprised (RR 205)

    The spectator sees nothing outside of the frame. In interpreting this shot, then, there would be nothing wrong with speculating that George and the policeman were looking at a robbery taking place across the street, or for that matter, at the cameraman, or a moviegoer in the left side of the theater. But the establishing shot and the dialogue serve to reinforce continuity from shot to shot, ensuring that we interpret the glances as falling inside the diegetic space.

    The next shot is the eyeline match. This shot shows us what the two men were staring at, and confirms our construction of story space. This is a relatively straightforward example. The eyeline match engages our expectations even more powerfully when employed without an establishing shot.

    These types of continuity devices are concerned with establishing an unambiguous, easily interpreted diegetic space that allows the viewer to concentrate on the progress of the story. The eyeline match, by encouraging the viewer to interpret offscreen glances as occuring completely within the diegesis, serve to maximize diegetic effect.

    There is another type of device which smooths the transition between cuts, that of graphic matching. Here, the narrative center of interest at successive shots is made to fall roughly in the same part of the screen, to minimize the effort the viewer needs to pick up the linear narrative thread.

    Axis of Surface/Depth:

    We move now to the question of surface/depth. The composition of an illusion of a natural, 3-dimensional space is essential to maximizing the diegetic effect, i.e. to achieve the illusion of a story world on a 2-dimensional screen.

    We have discussed the establishing shot, 180° line of action, the eyeline match, and the graphic match as devices that work for continuity. The problem of overcoming the artificuality and "flat" feel of the "theatrical shot" led to the development of other techniques which were also quickly codified into norms on the axis of surface/depth.

    First, one characteristic of the theatrical shot is that it is shot at a 90° angle to a back wall. Filmmakers soon realized that if the camera was placed at an oblique angle, the sense of depth was enhanced.


    Rudolf Valentino (BB 265)

    Note here the checkerboard pattern on the floor establishes a sense of perspective quite different from the centered vault of the theatrical shot.


    Fixtures above are lighting diffusers (CH 20.15)

    Another obstacle to the convincing establishment of the diegetic world is the fact that studio sets are artificial. For example, most did not have ceilings. This was solved simply by not filming the ceiling. Again, while this seems like an obvious, even necessary choice, our study of Japanese film will show us that it is only one choice among many.

    These codified techniques serve to support the maximization of diegetic effect both by constructing an illusion of a 3-dimensional story world, and by calling attention away from the production process, and the 2-dimensional nature of the screen.

    Axis of Centered/Decentered:

    So far we have been discussing Burch's contention that the modes of representation of Hollywood realist cinema were developed in order to support a method of storytelling that strives for maximum diegetic cinema were developed in order to support a method of storytelling that strives for maximum diegetic effect in a linear, charater-centered narrative. We brought up three "axes" which can be seen as choices with which the emerging cinema was faced as it developed its norms of representation.

    1. continuity/discontinuity: Hollywood realist cinema emphasis continuity to achieve its goals of maximum story illusion.
    2. depth/surface: We explored the several devices which realist cinema uses to promote the illusion of a 3-dimensional story space on the 2-dimensional screen.
    3. centered/decentered: Burch identifies this third axis along which Hollywood realist cinema based its mode of representation.

    Insofar as a mode of representation constantly directs attention to the human center of interest of a linear narrative, i.e. to the part of the diegetic world containing the most important information for the progress of the narrative, it may be said to be centering the narrative information. As you may expect, since Western cinema [Hollywood Realist Cinema] stresses the linear comprehension of a character-based narrative, it will work for a high degree of centering.


    A Temperamental Wife (1919) (CH 17.7)

    The simplest technique for centering is to place the narrative center of interest in the center of the frame.

    In Hollywood cinema, characters involved in the narrative may stray close to the edge of the frame, but their faces may never be intersected by the frame edge.

If we consider this in light of the axis of centering/decentering, we can answer the question, crucial to Burch's argument about Japanese film, of what the pointing function of these devices is being used for, of what the spectator's attention is being drawn to.     The privileging of narrative information is absolutely critical to the Hollywood realist effect, and filmmakers use every device at their disposal to direct our attention to the human center of interest. Lighting and point-of-view shots are just two other examples. These devices were codified early in the history of Hollywood cinema to support linearity and the human character as the center of narrative.

    Review:

    One way to interpret the title of To the Distant Observer is to note that Burch never claims to be interested in understanding Japanese film from the point of view of a Japanese spectator or critic. Japanese film is privileged here as an object in which is inscribed a critique of the Hollywood codes for the Western critic. This is why Burch studies Japanese film.

    This unit is meant to present "Western cinema" as Burch constructs it. What we have seen is that the modes of representation of mainstream realist cinema, which are so familiar to us through movies and TV that they seem natural, actually work hard to bring about three effects which we have called characteristics of mainstream realist cinema:

    1. Maximization and generalization of the diegetic effect, or the illusion of the story world and its corollary, the masking of film's status as a product, signifying process, commodity, and public spectacle.
    2. Linearity, by which is meant that the scene unfolds once, in one direction, and that the spectator's attention is always directed to the most narratively central part of the frame.
    3. Anthropocentric, character-based causality.

    Early in the history of Hollywood cinema, the modes of representation which work to support these characteristics were codified in three areas or axes:

    1. Axis of continuity/discontinuity: mainstream cinema strives for seamless continuity in order to enhance linear comprehension of the narrative, minimize spatio-temporal ambiguity, and establish a homogeneous 3-dimensional space.
    2. Axis of surface/depth: mainstream cinema strives for an illusion of depth to maximize the diegetic effect, and to take attention away from the 2-dimensional quality of the screen image
    3. Axis of centered/decentered: mainstream cinema strives to center diegetic narrative information to enhance linear comprehension of the narrative, and place the non-diegetic world, including the signifying process itself, off the screen.

    These norms may appear to us as the most natural way to tell a story with film, and this is not surprising since the vast majority of Hollywood movies, as well as TV shows, and even commercials that we see in our lives adhere quite closely to the mainstream style. You can check this tonight by watching a few TV programs.

    Burch studies Japanese film precisely to question the "naturalness" or necessity of these choices. In the next two units, we will see in Japanese cinema both a general indifference to these powerful norms of representation and style, and, in a handful of auteurs such as Ozu and Mizoguchi, we will find highly refined, fully developed alternatives to the mainstream style.
